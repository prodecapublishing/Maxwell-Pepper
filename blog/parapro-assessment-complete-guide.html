What Is the ParaPro Assessment?

The ETS ParaPro Assessment is a standardized test required for paraprofessional certification in most US school districts. Paraprofessionals — also called instructional aides, teaching assistants, or paraeducators — are required by No Child Left Behind (and subsequent legislation) to demonstrate basic skills in Reading, Writing, and Mathematics.

The test contains 90 multiple-choice questions divided equally across three sections. It is administered at Prometric testing centers and takes 2.5 hours.

The 3 Test Sections Explained

Reading (30 questions):

  • 18 questions on reading content knowledge — main ideas, vocabulary in context, text structure, inferences
  • 12 questions on application to classroom — helping students comprehend text, identify literary elements, use context clues

Writing (30 questions):

  • 18 questions on writing content knowledge — grammar, usage, punctuation, capitalization, sentence structure
  • 12 questions on application to classroom — identifying student writing errors, suggesting improvements, supporting writing instruction

Mathematics (30 questions):

  • 18 questions on math content knowledge — number sense, operations, fractions, decimals, percentages, basic algebra, geometry, data interpretation
  • 12 questions on application to classroom — explaining math procedures, identifying student errors, supporting math instruction

Scoring: What Score Do You Need?

ETS reports ParaPro scores on a scale of 420 to 480. Passing scores vary by state and employer — typically ranging from 455 to 465. Check with your school district or state education agency for the specific score required in your area.

There is no official pass rate published by ETS. Based on candidate reports, the test is considered moderately challenging — most prepared candidates pass on their first attempt, but the math section without a calculator trips up candidates who have not reviewed arithmetic in years.

What Is Actually Hard About the ParaPro?

The math section, without a calculator. The ParaPro explicitly prohibits calculators. If you have been relying on a calculator for everyday arithmetic for the past decade, you will feel the pain on questions involving fractions, percentages, and multi-step word problems.

The "application to classroom" questions. These are not just knowledge questions — they ask you to apply your knowledge in a teaching context. For example: "A student reads a paragraph and gives the wrong main idea. What is the most appropriate response?" These require you to think about instruction, not just the subject matter itself.

Grammar nuances. The writing section tests subject-verb agreement, pronoun agreement, modifier placement, and comma usage at a level that catches many candidates who are confident in their everyday writing but not in formal grammar rules.

A 2-Week Study Plan

Days 1-3: Reading section review. Focus on identifying main ideas, tone, and inference questions. Practice with short passages and timed question sets.

Days 4-6: Writing section review. Review the grammar rules that are most commonly tested: subject-verb agreement, pronoun reference, misplaced modifiers, comma usage, and run-on sentences.

Days 7-10: Mathematics review. Work through arithmetic, fractions, percents, basic algebra, and geometry without a calculator. Focus especially on word problems — the format most commonly tested.

Days 11-14: Full practice tests under timed conditions. Review every wrong answer and identify whether errors come from content knowledge or application-to-classroom questions. Target remaining time at your weakest area.

Math Without a Calculator: Tips for the Hardest Section

These techniques help you work faster and more accurately on the calculator-free math section:

  • Estimation: On many multiple-choice questions, you do not need an exact answer — just close enough to eliminate wrong choices
  • Fraction shortcuts: Practice converting fractions to decimals mentally (1/4 = 0.25, 1/3 = 0.333, 3/8 = 0.375)
  • Percentage shortcuts: 10% is always easy; 20% is double of 10%; 5% is half of 10%
  • Elimination: On word problems, eliminate answers that are obviously too large or too small before calculating
  • Re-read the question: Many errors come from solving for the wrong thing — always confirm what the question is actually asking
