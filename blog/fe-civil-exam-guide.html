What Is the FE Civil Exam?

The FE Civil exam (Fundamentals of Engineering — Civil discipline) is administered by NCEES and is the first step toward Professional Engineer (PE) licensure. It is a 5-hour and 20-minute computer-based exam containing 110 questions across two 55-question sessions, with a 25-minute break in between.

Uniquely among professional exams, the FE Civil is open book — candidates have access to the FE Reference Handbook (provided digitally on the testing computer). However, this does not make it easy. Knowing how to navigate the handbook quickly under time pressure is itself a significant skill the exam tests.

The Open Book Strategy: Using the Handbook Effectively

The FE Reference Handbook is available for free download from NCEES at ncees.org. You should have it open every time you study. The goal is not to memorize formulas — it is to know exactly where every formula is in the handbook so you can find it in under 30-45 seconds during the exam.

Effective handbook study means:

  • Bookmarking the sections for each knowledge area you study
  • Doing every practice problem by looking up the formula in the handbook, not from memory
  • Building a personal index of which page each key formula is on
  • Practicing until navigating the handbook is automatic

Candidates who try to memorize formulas instead of learning the handbook fail at a much higher rate than those who invest in handbook navigation skills.

All FE Civil Knowledge Areas

The FE Civil exam covers these knowledge areas (NCEES does not publish exact question counts per area):

  • Mathematics and Statistics
  • Ethics and Professional Practice
  • Engineering Economics
  • Statics
  • Dynamics
  • Mechanics of Materials
  • Materials
  • Fluid Mechanics
  • Surveying
  • Water Resources and Environmental Engineering
  • Structural Analysis
  • Structural Design
  • Geotechnical Engineering
  • Transportation Engineering
  • Construction

Based on candidate reports, Geotechnical Engineering, Structural Analysis, and Water Resources tend to have the most questions. Mathematics and Ethics are lighter sections.

Pass Rates and Why Candidates Fail

NCEES reports an overall FE Civil pass rate of approximately 65-70% for first-time US graduates. The most common failure causes:

  • Poor handbook navigation: Spending 3-4 minutes finding a formula on a single question destroys your time budget across the whole exam
  • Weak geotechnical or fluid mechanics knowledge: These are heavily tested and many candidates underestimate them
  • Running out of time: At 110 questions in 220 minutes, you have exactly 2 minutes per question on average — including looking up formulas
  • Not doing enough practice problems: Reading about engineering concepts does not build the problem-solving fluency the exam requires

A 10-Week Study Plan

Weeks 1-2: Mathematics, Statics, and Mechanics of Materials. These are foundational — the math and statics knowledge underlies almost every other section.

Weeks 3-4: Dynamics, Fluid Mechanics, and Water Resources. Fluid mechanics is heavily tested and conceptually challenging. Invest serious time here.

Weeks 5-6: Structural Analysis, Structural Design, and Geotechnical Engineering. Geotechnical is where many candidates are most rusty.

Weeks 7-8: Transportation Engineering, Surveying, Construction, and Engineering Economics. These are smaller sections but worth solid coverage.

Weeks 9-10: Full-length timed practice exams. Review wrong answers. Identify your 3 weakest areas and spend remaining time drilling those specifically with handbook navigation.

Common Mistakes That Cost Engineers the Exam

Studying without the handbook open. If you are not looking up every formula in the handbook during your study sessions, you are building the wrong habit for exam day.

Only reviewing material from your specialty. Most civil engineering graduates are strong in their concentration (structural, environmental, transportation) but weak in others. The exam does not care about your specialty.

Waiting too long after graduation. The longer you wait after graduation, the more the foundational mathematics erodes. Most successful candidates sit within 1-2 years of graduating.

Not building time awareness. Take at least 2-3 timed full-length practice sessions before exam day. Time management is a skill that requires practice under pressure.
